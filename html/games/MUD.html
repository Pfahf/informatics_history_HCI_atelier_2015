MUD

Colossal Cave Adventure

A MUD is a sub-genre of the Role-playing video game category. It stands for Multi-User Dungeon, which means that these types of games consist of dungeon crawling, role-playing, level interaction and sometimes there is also some player versus player. These MUD games were mainly played on computers as text-based games, which is a game where the player interacts by writing actions to perform on the computer like north or attack. On the computer screen is described the location of the player and the situation he is in with multiple possible actions he can perform.

Since these games are inspired by the the fantasy tabletop role-playing game calles Dungeons & Dragons (1974), they tend to mostly be fantasy themed. Others can be sci-fy themed or based on books and popular movies. The goal of these games may vary but they are mainly about slaying monsters, exploring dungeons, completing quests and so forth.

The history of MUD

Zork

MUD games go back to 1975 where Colossal Cave Adventure was created by Will Crowther on a DEC PDP-10 computer. It became very popular as an adventure game that was later quickly followed by another MUD by Crowther called Adventure. This marks the beginning of MUD's because after the release of Adventure, a group of MIT students that were inspired by this game wrote in 1977 a game called Zork. It quickly gained in popularity on the ARPANET and became one of the most important MUD game. The creators of this game later founded Infocom with other programmers and released a series of MUD games mainly Zork sequels like Zork III: The Dungeon Master (1982).

However while Infocom released more and more games, a student in Essex University in the UK called Roy Trubshaw not only defined this sub-genre by creating a game called MUD, but with the help from Richard Bartle, created in 1980, the first multiplayer online role-playing game that ran on the University's network. After this event MUD games began being commercialised and ported into other systems. Another trend later came which consisted of gaming sites, where people could retrieve MUD game codes to play them on their computers. An example is the site called Gamers World that was created by Mark Jacobs in 1984. In this site you could retrieve game codes to play MUD's like Aradath (1985). Until 1991, MUD games just kept gaining in popularity for their online factor and their text-based adventures inspired by famous books like J. R. R. Tolkien's The Hobbit (1937) or The Lord of the Rings series.

Different types of MUD's

Like I said before, not every MUD game are fantasy based, not only do the themes vary but also the gameplay and the game types. The most common game type being the basic Hack and Slash. It involves slaying monsters while exploring dungeons or other areas which almost always contains a boss that you need to beat in order proceed. In these games there was usually some looting and experience gaining in order to level up to upgrade stats like attack or health. Another popular game style was player versus player where players would fight each other in order to win. These games were mainly found in University campuses because they were played on the University's network. Then there was a style that started in the 1980s called graphical MUD's. They were basically MUD's that contained actual pixel art instead of just text on a screen. The player would then be able to see his environment however this trend just started with the game Habitat but this style didn't get much attention for a decade. Other game styles are:

  • Roleplaying MUD's where the player would role play a character.
  • Social MUD's where players would interact with each other
  • Educational MUD's where the game would be created to educate the player
These were the most common MUD's, there were however more because these types of games aren't contained in a set category or theme.
